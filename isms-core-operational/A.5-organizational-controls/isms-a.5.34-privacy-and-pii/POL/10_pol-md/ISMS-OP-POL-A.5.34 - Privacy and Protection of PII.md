# Data Protection Policy

## Purpose

The purpose of this policy is to define the organisation's legal and regulatory requirements under the Swiss nFADP (revDSG) and the Data Protection Ordinance (DSV), and to protect the rights of data subjects.

This policy supports Swiss nFADP (revDSG) by implementing technical and organisational measures appropriate to risk to protect personal data (including sensitive personal data). Where the organisation processes data of individuals in the EU/EEA, GDPR requirements also apply.

## Scope

All employees and third-party users.
Personal data as defined by Swiss nFADP and, where applicable, GDPR.

## Principle

Personal data is classified and treated as classification level Confidential, and all associated policies, controls, and processes apply.

---

## Data Protection Policy Statement

The organisation shall determine its role as **Data Controller**, **Data Processor**, or **Joint Controller** for each processing activity based on the context of its processing activities under the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP/revDSG). This determination shall be documented in the Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) and reflected in contractual arrangements with third parties. This policy confirms our commitment to protect the privacy of the personal information of our customers, clients, employees, and other interested parties. We have engaged in a programme of Information Security Management aligned to the international standard ISO 27001 to ensure that the processing of personal information is conducted using best practice processes.

## Legal Basis for Processing

Under Swiss nFADP, personal data may be processed provided that data protection principles are respected (Art. 6 nFADP). Unlike GDPR, a specific legal basis is not required for each processing activity unless processing involves sensitive personal data, high-risk profiling, or where consent is otherwise required by law.

Where the organisation processes data of EU/EEA individuals, a valid legal basis under GDPR Article 6 shall be identified and documented for each processing activity in the Record of Processing Activities.

# Data Protection Principles

The organisation is committed to processing data in accordance with its responsibilities under the Swiss nFADP and, where applicable, the EU GDPR.

Article 6 of the nFADP requires that personal data shall be:

## Lawfulness and Good Faith

- Processed lawfully and in good faith.

- The organisation has reviewed and documented the data that it controls and/or processes and determined the legal basis for processing.

### Duty to Inform (Privacy Notices)

The organisation shall inform data subjects at the time of data collection (Art. 19 nFADP). Privacy notices shall be provided covering:

- The identity and contact details of the controller.
- The purposes of processing.
- The categories of personal data processed.
- Recipients or categories of recipients.
- Whether data is transferred abroad, and the applicable safeguards.
- The retention period or criteria for determining it.
- The rights of the data subject (access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection).
- Contact details of the Data Protection Advisor (if appointed).

Privacy notices shall be concise, transparent, and written in plain language. They shall be made available at the point of data collection (e.g., on websites, in application forms, in employment contracts, at reception for visitors).

### Consent Management

Where consent is relied upon as the basis for processing (e.g., marketing communications, optional analytics, or processing of sensitive personal data), the following requirements apply:

- Consent shall be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous (Art. 6(6)–(7) nFADP).
- Consent for sensitive personal data or high-risk profiling shall be explicit (Art. 6(7) nFADP).
- Consent shall be recorded with the date, method, and scope of consent granted.
- Withdrawal of consent shall be as easy as giving consent, and the process shall be communicated to data subjects.
- Systems shall be capable of recording and actioning consent withdrawal without undue delay.
- Consent records shall be retained for the duration of processing plus the applicable limitation period.

## Purpose Limitation

- Collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes.

The organisation ensures it only processes data for the purposes it has been collected and communicated, and not for other reasons without the agreement and knowledge of the data subject(s).

## Data Minimisation

- Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed.

The organisation ensures that data collected is not excessive and is appropriate to the purpose for which it was collected. Data Protection Impact Assessments are conducted as part of the project lifecycle for high-risk processing.

## Accuracy

- Accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date; every reasonable step shall be taken to ensure that personal data that are inaccurate, having regard to the purposes for which they are processed, are erased or rectified without delay.

- The organisation ensures that data is reviewed and assessed for accuracy on a periodic basis and has implemented processes for the rectification and erasure of data without undue delay.

## Storage Period Limitation

- Kept in a form which permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is necessary for the purposes for which the personal data are processed.

The organisation has implemented a data retention policy and data retention schedule in line with legal, regulatory, and business needs. Personal data shall be destroyed or anonymised once it is no longer needed for the stated processing purpose (Art. 6(4) nFADP).

## Security of Processing

- Processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal data, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction, or damage, using appropriate technical or organisational measures.

The organisation has implemented an information security management system in line with ISO 27001, the international standard for information security. Security controls and requirements are assessed throughout the project lifecycle.

# Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)

The organisation shall maintain a Record of Processing Activities documenting all processing operations (DSV Art. 4–5).

**SME exemption (DSV Art. 4):** Organisations with fewer than 250 employees are exempted from maintaining a ROPA unless they process sensitive personal data on a large scale or conduct high-risk profiling. The organisation shall document its assessment of whether the exemption applies.

Where a ROPA is maintained, it shall include: purposes of processing, categories of data subjects and personal data, recipients, cross-border transfers, retention periods, and a description of technical and organisational security measures.

The ROPA shall be reviewed and updated annually, or whenever new processing activities commence, and made available to the FDPIC upon request.

# Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

The organisation shall conduct a DPIA before beginning processing activities that are likely to result in a high risk to the rights of data subjects (Art. 22 nFADP, Art. 35 GDPR where applicable).

A DPIA is required when processing involves:

- Large-scale processing of sensitive personal data (Art. 5(c) nFADP).
- Systematic and large-scale profiling.
- New technologies that may pose significant privacy risks.
- Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas on a large scale (GDPR Art. 35(3)).

Each DPIA shall document: a description of the processing and its purposes, an assessment of necessity and proportionality, an assessment of risks to data subjects, and measures to address those risks.

Where a qualified Data Protection Advisor has been appointed, they may approve the DPIA in place of consulting the FDPIC (Art. 23(4) nFADP). Where residual risk remains high and no Data Protection Advisor is appointed, the FDPIC shall be consulted.

DPIA documentation shall be retained for at least 2 years after processing ends (DSV Art. 14).

# Personal Information Classification and Handling

Personal data classification and handling is in line with the Information Classification and Handling Policy.

The nFADP defines the following categories requiring particular attention:

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Basic personal data** | Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person |
| **Sensitive personal data** (Art. 5(c) nFADP) | Religious, philosophical, political, or trade union opinions or activities; health data; intimate sphere data; racial or ethnic origin data; genetic data; biometric data; administrative and criminal proceedings or sanctions data; social security measures data |

# Personal Information Retention

Personal data is retained and destroyed in line with the Information Classification and Handling Policy, Asset Management Policy, and the Data Retention Schedule.

Retention periods shall be agreed by the relevant data owners in line with legal, regulatory, and business requirements:

| Data Type | Indicative Retention Period | Legal Basis |
|-----------|-----------------------------|-------------|
| Accounting and business records | 10 years | Swiss Code of Obligations (OR) Art. 958f |
| Tax records | 10 years | Swiss Tax Law |
| Employment records | 5 years after end of employment | OR Art. 127–128 (limitation period) |
| Contract documentation | 10 years | OR Art. 127 (general limitation) |
| DPIA documentation | At least 2 years after processing ends | DSV Art. 14 |

When the retention target is reached, the information shall be reviewed by the relevant data owner to confirm whether the information is to be further retained or destroyed. It shall be destroyed in line with the Information Classification and Handling Policy if there is no further business, statutory, or historical reason to retain it.

# Personal Information Transfer

Personal data is transferred in line with the Information Transfer Policy. Employees shall ensure the appropriate level of security in line with the policy and organisation processes.

## Cross-Border Data Transfers

The organisation shall not transfer personal data to countries outside Switzerland unless:

- The Swiss Federal Council has determined the country provides adequate data protection (Art. 16(1) nFADP, Annex 1 DSV), or
- Appropriate safeguards are in place (Standard Contractual Clauses adapted for Swiss law, Binding Corporate Rules approved by the FDPIC, or equivalent contractual safeguards), or
- A specific exception applies (explicit consent, contract performance, vital interests, or other derogation under Art. 17 nFADP).

Where the organisation also processes EU/EEA data, European Commission adequacy decisions and GDPR transfer mechanisms (Art. 44–49) shall be applied as appropriate.

# Personal Information Storage

Personal information storage is in line with the Information Classification and Handling Policy, Physical and Environmental Security Policy, Use of Cryptography Policy, Backup Policy, and the Data Retention Schedule.

# Breach

In the event of a breach of the data protection principles, employees shall inform their line manager and/or a member of the Management Review Team and invoke the Incident Management Process.

Breaches shall be reported as follows:

| Requirement | Swiss nFADP | EU GDPR (where applicable) |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------------|
| **Authority notification** | As soon as possible to the FDPIC (Art. 24 nFADP) — for breaches likely to result in high risk | Within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach to the competent supervisory authority (Art. 33) — for breaches likely to result in a risk |
| **Data subject notification** | When necessary for their protection, or when the FDPIC demands it (Art. 24(4)) | Without undue delay when high risk (Art. 34) |
| **Internal documentation** | All breaches shall be documented regardless of notification threshold |

The organisation shall maintain a breach register documenting the facts, effects, and remedial actions for all breaches.

# The Rights of Data Subjects

Under Swiss nFADP and, where applicable, GDPR, data subjects have the following rights:

## The Right of Access

- Data subjects have the right to request whether personal data concerning them is being processed.
- The organisation shall provide information on the purpose, categories of data, recipients, retention period, and source.
- Information shall be provided in writing, free of charge, within 30 days (Art. 25 nFADP). This period may be extended by a further 30 days where necessary due to complexity, with notification to the data subject.

## The Right to Rectification

- Data subjects have the right to have inaccurate personal data rectified or completed if it is incomplete.
- A request for rectification may be made verbally or in writing.
- The organisation shall respond within 30 days.

## The Right to Erasure

- Data subjects have the right to request erasure of personal data (Art. 32(2)(c) nFADP).
- The right is not absolute and only applies where there is no legal justification for continued retention.

## The Right to Data Portability

- Data subjects have the right to receive their personal data in a commonly used electronic format, or to request direct transfer to another controller (Art. 28 nFADP).
- This is a new right under the revised nFADP.

## The Right to Object

- Data subjects have the right to object to the processing of their personal data (Art. 30(2)(b) nFADP).
- The right to stop processing for direct marketing purposes is absolute.

## The Right to Information on Automated Decisions

- Data subjects have the right to be informed of decisions made solely by automated means that significantly affect them (Art. 21 nFADP).
- The data subject may request human review of such decisions.

# Definitions

To ensure the organisation understands its obligations to the protection of personal information, the following definitions apply:

## Personal Data

Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person ("Data Subject") who can be identified, directly or indirectly, by reference to an identifier such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier, or to one or more factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural, or social identity of that natural person. Under the revised nFADP, legal persons are no longer covered.

## Sensitive Personal Data

Personal data which, by their nature, require particular protection. Under nFADP Art. 5(c), this includes data on religious, philosophical, political, or trade union opinions or activities; health data; data on the intimate sphere; racial or ethnic origin data; genetic data; biometric data for unique identification; data on administrative and criminal proceedings or sanctions; and data on social security measures.

## Data Controller

The natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or any other body which alone or jointly with others determines the purposes and means of the processing of personal data.

## Data Processor

A natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or any other body which processes personal data on behalf of a data controller.

## Processing

An operation or set of operations performed on personal data, whether or not by automated means, such as collection, recording, organisation, structuring, storage, adaptation or alteration, retrieval, consultation, use, disclosure by transmission, dissemination or otherwise making available, alignment or combination, restriction, erasure, or destruction.

## Anonymisation

Irreversibly de-identifying personal data such that the person cannot be identified by using reasonable time, cost, and technology either by the controller or by any other person. Data protection principles do not apply to anonymised data as it is no longer personal data.

# Data Retention

## Agreement of Retention Periods

The relevant owners of the documentation as detailed in the asset register are responsible for agreeing the data retention periods in line with legal, regulatory, and business requirements. Data retention periods shall be approved by legal counsel.

## Record of Retention Periods

Retention periods are recorded in the Data Asset Register. Additional detail is contained, where applicable and appropriate, in the Record of Processing Activities and the Asset Register.

## Expiry of Retention Period

When the retention target is reached, the information shall be reviewed by the relevant owners to confirm that the information is to be further retained or destroyed. It shall be destroyed in line with the Information Classification and Handling Policy if there is no further business, statutory, or historical reason to retain it.

## Suspension of Record Disposal in the Event of Litigation or Claims

In the event any employee of the organisation reasonably anticipates or becomes aware of a governmental investigation or audit, or the commencement of any litigation, all further disposal of documents shall be suspended until management, with the advice of legal counsel, determines otherwise. Management shall take such steps as are necessary to promptly inform affected staff of any suspension in the disposal or destruction of documents.

## Children's Data

Where the organisation processes personal data of children (minors under 18 in Switzerland, under 16 in the EU/EEA where GDPR applies):

- Additional care shall be taken to ensure privacy notices are understandable by the child or their legal representative.
- Consent for online services directed at children shall be obtained from the holder of parental authority where the child is under the applicable age threshold.
- Processing shall be limited to what is strictly necessary and proportionate.

Where the organisation does not intentionally process children's data, this shall be documented. If children's data is discovered, it shall be assessed for compliance with applicable requirements.

## Optional: Data Protection Advisor (nFADP Art. 10)

*The appointment of a Data Protection Advisor is **voluntary** for private organisations under Swiss nFADP (unlike the mandatory DPO requirement under GDPR Art. 37 for certain processing activities). However, appointment is **strongly recommended** as it provides tangible benefits.*

If a Data Protection Advisor is appointed, they shall:

- Perform their function independently and without being bound by instructions.
- Not perform activities incompatible with their advisory role.
- Possess the necessary professional knowledge.
- Have their contact details published and communicated to the FDPIC.

**Key benefit**: Appointing a qualified Data Protection Advisor exempts the organisation from consulting the FDPIC on DPIAs with residual high risk (Art. 23(4) nFADP).

Where the organisation also processes EU/EEA data and meets the criteria in GDPR Art. 37 (e.g., large-scale processing of special categories, systematic monitoring), a Data Protection Officer (DPO) appointment is mandatory under GDPR.

# Policy Compliance

## Compliance Measurement

The information security management team will verify compliance with this policy through various methods, including but not limited to, ROPA reviews, DSR response audits, breach register reviews, internal and external audits, and feedback to the policy owner.

## Exceptions

Any exception to this policy shall be approved and recorded by the Information Security Manager in advance, with documented risk acceptance, compensating controls, and a defined review date. Exceptions shall be reported to the Management Review Team.

Exceptions shall not be granted for mandatory regulatory requirements under nFADP or GDPR.

## Non-Compliance

An employee found to have violated this policy may be subject to disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment. Under nFADP, individuals responsible for wilful data protection violations may be subject to criminal fines of up to CHF 250,000.

## Continual Improvement

This policy is reviewed and updated as part of the continual improvement process. Reviews shall consider changes to data protection legislation, supervisory authority guidance, emerging privacy risks, and lessons learned from incidents.

# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## What This Assessment Does

**ISMS-IMP-A.8.16.4** assesses ALERT MANAGEMENT and RESPONSE EFFECTIVENESS - answering the critical question: **"Are we actually RESPONDING to alerts, or just generating noise?"**

**The Alert Management Problem:**
Most organisations generate thousands of alerts daily:

- 80-90% are false positives (benign activity misclassified)
- 5-10% are true positives requiring investigation
- 1-2% are critical incidents requiring immediate response
- Many alerts never investigated (queue overflow, alert fatigue)

**This assessment evaluates:**
1. **Alert Lifecycle:** Creation → Triage → Investigation → Escalation → Resolution
2. **Response Timeliness:** Are SLAs met? (Critical: <1hr, High: <4hr, Medium: <24hr)
3. **Investigation Quality:** Are investigations thorough or superficial "close ticket" exercises?
4. **Escalation Effectiveness:** Do critical alerts reach the right people quickly?
5. **Alert Quality:** False positive rates, tuning effectiveness, alert value

## Why This Assessment Matters

**The Alert Management Crisis:**

Without effective alert management, monitoring infrastructure is USELESS:

- Generate 10,000 alerts/day → No one investigates → Real attacks missed
- Critical alert buried in noise → Discovered weeks later during forensics
- Alert fatigue → Analysts ignore alerts → "Boy who cried wolf" problem
- No SLA tracking → Response times unknown → Incidents escalate unchecked

**This assessment prevents:**

- **Alert fatigue:** Overwhelming analysts with false positives
- **Missed attacks:** Critical alerts lost in noise
- **Slow response:** No defined SLAs or tracking
- **Process gaps:** Undefined escalation, investigation, closure procedures
- **Compliance failures:** Unable to demonstrate response capability

## Alert Management Maturity Levels

**Level 1 - Reactive (Firefighting):**

- Alerts generated but no formal process
- Investigations ad-hoc, no documentation
- No SLA tracking
- High false positive rate (>50%)
- Critical alerts missed regularly

**Level 2 - Managed (Process Defined):**

- Alert triage process exists
- Some SLA tracking (manual)
- Investigation procedures documented
- False positive rate improving (30-50%)
- Most critical alerts investigated

**Level 3 - Optimized (Proactive):**

- Automated alert triage and enrichment
- SLA tracking with dashboards
- Playbook-driven investigations
- False positive rate <30%
- 100% critical alert investigation within SLA

**Level 4 - Continuous Improvement (Data-Driven):**

- Alert quality metrics tracked
- Continuous tuning based on FP/TP rates
- Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) improving
- Automated response for common scenarios
- False positive rate <20%

**This assessment identifies your current maturity level and gaps to next level.**

# Assessment Overview

## Purpose & Scope

**Assessment Name:** ISMS-IMP-A.8.16.4 - Alert Management & Response Assessment

This assessment evaluates the EFFECTIVENESS of your alert handling processes from initial alert generation through final resolution.

**Core Purpose:**
Ensure alerts generated by monitoring infrastructure (A.8.16.1) and detection rules (A.8.16.2) are:

- **Triaged promptly** (classified by severity and urgency)
- **Investigated thoroughly** (root cause determined, context gathered)
- **Escalated appropriately** (critical alerts reach decision-makers)
- **Resolved completely** (actions taken, findings documented)
- **Continuously improved** (false positives reduced, processes refined)

**Scope Boundaries:**

**IN SCOPE:**

- Alert lifecycle from creation to closure
- Alert triage and prioritization procedures
- Investigation workflows and playbooks
- Escalation criteria and procedures
- Response time SLAs and tracking
- Alert quality metrics (FP/TP rates)
- Integration with incident management

**OUT OF SCOPE:**

- Detection rule creation (covered in A.8.16.2)
- Monitoring infrastructure performance (covered in A.8.16.1)
- Forensic analysis procedures (covered in incident response plan)
- Post-incident review (covered in incident management process)

## Key Principle: Alerts Must Be Actionable

An alert is ONLY valuable if:
1. **Someone sees it** (not lost in queue overflow)
2. **Someone investigates it** (not ignored due to fatigue)
3. **Investigation is timely** (before incident escalates)
4. **Actions are taken** (alert drives security improvements)
5. **Results are documented** (evidence for audit, learning)

**Non-Actionable Alert = Noise**

If an alert:

- Has >50% false positive rate → Tune or disable
- Is never investigated → Disable (generating waste)
- Has no clear investigation procedure → Create playbook
- Takes >1 hour to investigate routine cases → Automate

- Alert count by status (Open, In Progress, Resolved, False Positive)

**How to Extract:**
```spl
# Splunk example
index=notable earliest=-90d latest=now
| stats count by date_mday, severity, rule_name, status
| outputlookup alert_volume_90d.csv
```

**Alert Timing Data:**

- Alert creation timestamp
- Triage timestamp (when analyst started investigating)
- Investigation completion timestamp
- Escalation timestamp (if escalated)
- Resolution timestamp
- Time in each stage (calculated durations)

**How to Extract:**
```spl
# Alert lifecycle timing
index=notable earliest=-90d latest=now
| eval triage_time = triage_timestamp - alert_timestamp
| eval investigation_time = resolved_timestamp - triage_timestamp
| eval total_time = resolved_timestamp - alert_timestamp
| stats avg(triage_time) as avg_triage, 
        avg(investigation_time) as avg_investigation,
        avg(total_time) as avg_total
        by severity
```

**Alert Disposition Data:**

- True Positive count (real security events)
- False Positive count (benign activity misclassified)
- Benign Positive count (policy violations, not security threats)
- Indeterminate count (insufficient data to determine)

   - Procedures documented vs. undefined

## Sheet 2: Alert Volume & Source Analysis

**Purpose:** Understand alert volume, trends, and sources

**Policy Reference:** ISMS-POL-A.8.16, Section 2.3.1 (Alert Prioritization)

**Why This Sheet Matters:**
You can't manage what you don't measure. Without alert volume data:

- Can't assess if SOC capacity is adequate
- Can't identify which detection rules generate excessive noise
- Can't prioritize tuning efforts
- Can't detect alert volume trends (improving or degrading)

**Structure:**

- **Rows 8-32:** 25 data entry rows (alert sources)
- **Rows 35-55:** Alert volume analysis summary

### Column-by-Column Completion Guide

**Column A: Alert Source / Detection Rule ID**

- **What to Enter:** Detection rule that generates alerts
- **Examples:**
  - `DET-AUTH-001` (Failed Login Threshold)
  - `DET-MALWARE-005` (EDR Malware Detection)
  - `DET-NETWORK-010` (Unusual Outbound Traffic)
  - `IDS Signature 2024381` (SQL Injection Attempt)
- **Cross-Reference:** Should match rule IDs from A.8.16.2, Sheet 5

**Column B: Alert Source Name**

- **What to Enter:** Human-readable rule name
- **Examples:**
  - `Failed Login Attempts Exceeding Threshold`
  - `Malware Detected by EDR`
  - `Unusual Outbound Data Transfer Volume`

**Column C: Alert Type (Dropdown)**

- **Options:** Authentication, Malware, Network Anomaly, Data Exfiltration, Privilege Escalation, Policy Violation, System Anomaly, Application Error, Other
- **Purpose:** Group alerts by category for analysis

**Column D: Default Severity (Dropdown)**

- **Options:** Critical, High, Medium, Low, Informational
- **Selection:** What severity does this rule assign by default?

**Column E: Alert Volume (Last 30 Days)**

- **What to Enter:** Count of alerts from this source in last 30 days
- **How to Get:**

```spl
index=notable earliest=-30d latest=now
| stats count by rule_id, rule_name
| sort -count
```

**Column F: Alert Volume (Last 90 Days)**

- **Purpose:** Longer trend analysis

**Column G: Daily Average**

- **Auto-Calculated:** `= Column E / 30` or `= Column F / 90`
- **Purpose:** Normalize volume for comparison

**Column H: Trend (Dropdown)**

- **Options:** 📈 Increasing, 📉 Decreasing, ➡️ Stable, 🆕 New Rule
- **How to Determine:**
  - Compare Month 1 vs. Month 2 vs. Month 3
  - Increasing if Month 3 > Month 2 > Month 1 (by >20%)
  - Decreasing if Month 3 < Month 2 < Month 1 (by >20%)
  - Stable if variation <20%

**Column I: True Positives (TP)**

- **What to Enter:** Count of alerts that were real security events
- **Example:** 50 alerts, 10 were actual attacks → TP = 10

**Column J: False Positives (FP)**

- **What to Enter:** Count of alerts that were benign activity
- **Example:** 50 alerts, 35 were benign → FP = 35

**Column K: Indeterminate**

- **What to Enter:** Count of alerts where determination unclear
- **Example:** 50 alerts, 5 couldn't determine → Indeterminate = 5

**Column L: TP Rate (%)**

- **Auto-Calculated:** `= TP / (TP + FP + Indeterminate) × 100`
- **Example:** 10 / 50 × 100 = 20%

**Column M: FP Rate (%)**

- **Auto-Calculated:** `= FP / (TP + FP + Indeterminate) × 100`
- **Example:** 35 / 50 × 100 = 70%

**Column N: Alert Quality Status (Dropdown)**

- **Options:** ✅ Excellent, ⚠️ Acceptable, ❌ Needs Tuning, 🚫 Disable
- **Selection Logic:**
  - ✅ Excellent: TP Rate >50%, FP Rate <20%
  - ⚠️ Acceptable: TP Rate 30-50%, FP Rate 20-40%
  - ❌ Needs Tuning: TP Rate <30%, FP Rate >40%
  - 🚫 Disable: TP Rate <10%, FP Rate >70% (generating waste)

**Column O: Tuning Actions Needed**

- **What to Enter:** Specific tuning recommendations
- **Examples:**
  - `Increase threshold from 5 to 10 failed logins (reduce FP)`
  - `Add whitelist for backup service accounts (exclude benign)`
  - `Refine time-based threshold (business hours vs. off-hours)`
  - `Disable rule - no TP in 90 days, high FP`

**Column P: Tuning Priority (Dropdown)**

- **Options:** P0 (Immediate), P1 (This Sprint), P2 (Next Quarter), P3 (Backlog)
- **Selection:**
  - **P0:** Critical severity + FP Rate >70%
  - **P1:** High severity + FP Rate >50% OR High volume (>100/day) + FP >40%
  - **P2:** Medium severity + FP Rate >50%
  - **P3:** Low priority rules

**Column Q: Tuning Owner**

- **Who:** Person responsible for tuning this rule
- **Examples:** `SOC Detection Engineering Team`, `Jane Doe, Sr. Analyst`

**Column R: Notes**

- **Use Cases:**
  - **Tuning History:** "Threshold increased from 5 to 8 on 15.01.2026 - FP reduced from 60% to 35%"
  - **Context:** "High FP during business hours, acceptable off-hours - implement time-aware threshold"
  - **Exclusions:** "Whitelisted backup service account svc_backup - reduced FP by 40%"

# Common Pitfalls

## Pitfall 1: Alert Volume Exceeds SOC Capacity

**The Mistake:**
Generate 10,000 alerts/day → 3 SOC analysts → 3,333 alerts per analyst → Impossible.

**Reality Check:**
If analysts can't keep up with alert volume:

- Alerts sit unaddressed (backlog grows)
- Analysts triage superficially ("looks fine, close")
- Critical alerts missed (buried in noise)

**How to Avoid:**
**Calculate Capacity:**
```
Alerts per Day: 500
SOC Analysts (per shift): 2
Alerts per Analyst: 250/day

Investigation Time: 15 min/alert (average)
Analyst Capacity: 8 hours × 4 alerts/hour = 32 alerts/day

Capacity: 32 alerts/day × 2 analysts = 64 alerts/day
Demand: 500 alerts/day

GAP: 500 - 64 = 436 alerts/day UNADDRESSED ❌
```

**Solutions:**
1. **Reduce Alert Volume:** Tune high-FP rules (target: <30% FP)
2. **Automate Triage:** SOAR auto-closes known-good patterns
3. **Increase Capacity:** Hire more analysts OR extend coverage hours
4. **Prioritize:** Focus on Critical/High, defer Low

---

## Pitfall 2: No Documented Investigation Procedures

**The Mistake:**
Analyst A investigates thoroughly, Analyst B closes quickly → Inconsistent quality.

**Reality Check:**
Without playbooks:

- Quality depends on analyst (experienced vs. new)
- Tribal knowledge lost when analysts leave
- No quality baseline (can't measure improvement)

**How to Avoid:**

- Create playbooks for top 10 alert types (covers 80% of volume)
- Document WHAT to check, WHERE to find data, HOW to determine TP/FP
- Train all analysts on playbooks
- Mandate playbook use (exception for complex edge cases)

**Playbook Template:**
```markdown
# Alert Type: Failed Authentication Threshold

## Investigation Steps:
1. **User Context:**

   - Is account active? (AD lookup)
   - Recent password change? (AD audit logs)
   - User's department and access level?

2. **Source Analysis:**

   - Source IP address - is it known user device?
   - GeoIP location - does it match user's expected location?
   - VPN connection? (check VPN logs)

3. **Pattern Analysis:**

   - Check A.8.16.2 baseline - is this unusual for this user?
   - Multiple users from same IP? (possible credential spray)
   - Successful login after failures? (compromise indicator)

4. **Threat Intelligence:**

   - Is source IP on threat feeds?
   - Known malicious infrastructure?

5. **Disposition:**

   - **TP:** Password guessing, credential spray, brute force → Disable account, force password reset, escalate
   - **FP:** Legitimate user forgot password → Close with note
   - **Tuning:** If FP due to threshold too low, recommend increase

6. **Documentation:**

   - Record findings in ticket
   - Link evidence (screenshots, log excerpts)
   - Document actions taken

```

## Pitfall 3: SLAs Are Aspirational, Not Measured

**The Mistake:**
"We respond to Critical alerts within 1 hour" → But no one tracks actual response times.

**Reality Check:**
Without measurement:

- Don't know if SLAs are met (could be consistently violated)
- Can't identify which alert types cause SLA misses
- Can't prove compliance to auditors

**How to Avoid:**

- **Automated SLA Tracking:** SIEM calculates time deltas (alert creation → triage → resolution)
- **Dashboard:** Real-time view of SLA compliance % by severity
- **Alerts on SLA Violations:** Notify SOC manager if Critical alert exceeds 1-hour SLA
- **Monthly Reporting:** SLA compliance % included in SOC metrics

**SIEM Query for SLA Tracking:**
```spl
index=notable
| eval triage_time = triage_timestamp - alert_timestamp
| eval resolution_time = resolution_timestamp - alert_timestamp
| eval sla_target = case(
    severity="Critical", 120,  # 2 hours
    severity="High", 480,      # 8 hours
    severity="Medium", 2880,   # 48 hours
    severity="Low", 43200)     # 30 days
| eval sla_met = if(resolution_time <= sla_target, "Yes", "No")
| stats count, perc(sla_met="Yes") as sla_compliance_pct by severity
```

## Pitfall 4: High False Positive Rate Ignored

**The Mistake:**
Detection rule generates 80% false positives → "It's just noisy" → No tuning → Alert fatigue sets in.

**Reality Check:**
High FP rate consequences:

- Analyst time wasted on FPs (investigating benign activity)
- Alert fatigue → Analysts stop investigating thoroughly
- Real attacks missed (buried in FP noise)

**FP Rate Thresholds:**

- **<20%:** ✅ Excellent (high-value alerts)
- **20-30%:** ⚠️ Acceptable (monitor, consider tuning)
- **30-50%:** ⚠️ Needs Tuning (significant waste)
- **>50%:** ❌ Critical (immediate tuning or disable)

**How to Avoid:**

- **Track FP Rate:** Calculate per detection rule monthly
- **Tuning Priority:** P0 = Critical severity + FP>50%, P1 = High + FP>50%
- **Tuning Techniques:**
  - Increase threshold (e.g., 5 failed logins → 10)
  - Add whitelist (exclude known-good IPs, accounts)
  - Time-aware thresholds (different limits business hours vs. off-hours)
  - Correlation (alert only if multiple indicators, not single event)

**Document Tuning:**
```
Rule: DET-AUTH-001 (Failed Login Threshold)
Original: Alert on >5 failed logins
FP Rate: 60% (backup accounts, users forgetting passwords)

Tuning Action (15.01.2026):

- Increased threshold to 10 failed logins
- Whitelisted backup service accounts (svc_backup, svc_monitor)
- Added time window: 10 failures in 5 minutes (not 10 failures in 24 hours)

Result:

- FP Rate: 60% → 25% ✅
- Alert volume: 100/day → 30/day
- TP Rate: 10% → 40%

```

## Pitfall 5: Escalation Procedures Never Tested

**The Mistake:**
Document escalation procedures → Never test → When real incident happens, procedures don't work.

**Reality Check:**
Untested escalation paths fail:

- Contact information outdated (person left company)
- PagerDuty not configured correctly
- CISO doesn't have SIEM access (can't review alerts during escalation)
- Legal team unaware they're in escalation chain

**How to Avoid:**

- **Annual Tabletop Exercise:** Simulate critical incident, walk through escalation
- **Quarterly Test:** Send test escalation (notify recipients it's a test)
- **After-Hours Test:** Verify on-call rotation works (test at 2 AM)
- **Update Contact Info:** Quarterly review of escalation contact list

**Tabletop Exercise Template:**
```
Scenario: Ransomware detected on 10 servers at 14:00 Friday

Exercise Flow:
1. SOC Tier 1 analyst detects ransomware alert (SIEM)
2. Tier 1 escalates to SOC Manager (test escalation path)
3. SOC Manager escalates to IR Team (test IR notification)
4. IR Team escalates to CISO (test executive notification)
5. CISO decides to invoke IR plan (test decision-making)

Questions to Answer:

- How long did each escalation take?
- Were all recipients reachable?
- Did everyone know their role?
- What information was missing?
- What would improve the process?

Document findings, update procedures.
```

## Pitfall 6: Indeterminate Alerts Not Followed Up

**The Mistake:**
Alert investigated, can't determine TP/FP → Close as "Indeterminate" → Never revisited.

**Reality Check:**
Indeterminate = "We don't know if we were attacked."

- May be early indicator of sophisticated attack (low signal)
- May need additional monitoring to determine
- Closing without follow-up = potential missed attack

**How to Avoid:**

- **Indeterminate Queue:** Separate queue for indeterminate alerts
- **Re-Investigation:** Review indeterminate alerts after 7/30 days (new data may clarify)
- **Enhanced Monitoring:** If indeterminate, deploy additional monitoring on affected asset
- **Escalate if Pattern:** Multiple indeterminate alerts same asset → Escalate for deep dive

**Example:**
```
Initial Alert: Unusual outbound traffic from server SRV-APP-05
Investigation: No obvious malware, no IOC matches, no baseline (newly deployed)
Disposition: Indeterminate (insufficient data)

Follow-Up (7 days later):

- Baseline now established (A.8.16.2)
- Review traffic against baseline
- Determine: Legitimate application behavior OR Anomalous (escalate)

```

## Pitfall 7: No Post-Resolution Review

**The Mistake:**
Close alert → Move to next alert → No lessons learned.

**Reality Check:**
Without post-resolution review:

- Same attack succeeds multiple times (don't learn from TPs)
- Same FP pattern repeats (don't tune after discovering pattern)
- Process improvements not identified

**How to Avoid:**

- **TP Post-Resolution:** Document what could be improved
  - Better detection rule? (alert sooner, before impact)
  - Response automation? (SOAR could have contained faster)
  - Additional monitoring? (attack stage we didn't see)
- **FP Post-Resolution:** Document tuning action
  - What whitelisting/threshold change would prevent recurrence?
  - Update detection rule immediately (don't defer)
- **Weekly Review Meeting:** SOC team reviews notable alerts from past week
  - Discuss challenging investigations
  - Share lessons learned
  - Identify systemic issues

---

## Pitfall 8: Treating All Severity Levels Equally

**The Mistake:**
Low priority alerts get same attention as Critical → Critical alerts delayed while analysts investigate Low noise.

**Reality Check:**
Alert severity = urgency + impact. Critical alerts MUST be prioritized.

**How to Avoid:**

- **Queue Prioritization:** Critical alerts go to top of queue (FIFO within severity)
- **Assignment Rules:** Critical auto-assigned to senior analysts
- **Notification Escalation:** Critical triggers immediate notification (phone, page)
- **SLA Enforcement:** Critical SLA = 2 hours (firm), Low SLA = 30 days (flexible)

**Don't Let Low-Severity Noise Block Critical Response.**

---

## Pitfall 9: Alert Management Metrics Not Reviewed by Management

**The Mistake:**
SOC tracks metrics → Reports generated → Management doesn't review → No accountability for improvement.

**Reality Check:**
If management doesn't review:

- No pressure to improve (SLA compliance, FP rates)
- No resource allocation (if over capacity, no additional hires)
- No strategic direction (are we getting better or worse?)

**How to Avoid:**

- **Monthly SOC Metrics Review:** Present to CISO/Security Manager
  - Alert volume trends
  - SLA compliance %
  - FP rate trends
  - Top alert sources (tuning priorities)
  - Capacity vs. demand
- **Quarterly Board Reporting:** Include key metrics in security dashboard
  - Demonstrate monitoring effectiveness
  - Show continuous improvement
  - Request resources if needed

---

## Pitfall 10: Alerts Generated But No One On Duty

**The Mistake:**
24/7 monitoring infrastructure → Alerts generated at 2 AM → No SOC coverage → Alerts sit unaddressed until 8 AM.

**Reality Check:**
If Critical alert fires at 2 AM and no one responds for 6 hours:

- Attack escalates unchecked
- Data exfiltration completes
- Ransomware encrypts entire network

**How to Avoid:**
**Coverage Options:**
1. **24/7 SOC:** In-house or outsourced MDR (Managed Detection & Response)
2. **On-Call Rotation:** Tier 2/3 analysts on-call after hours (PagerDuty)
3. **Follow-the-Sun:** Partner with SOC in different timezone (handoff coverage)
4. **Automated Response:** SOAR handles routine alerts after hours (containment, notification)

**Minimum Requirement:** Critical alerts MUST trigger immediate notification (phone, page) to on-call responder, even if 2 AM.

# Review & Approval

**Three-Level Approval Workflow:**

**Level 1: Technical Review** (SOC Manager, Lead Analyst)

- **Focus:** Alert data accuracy, process documentation completeness
- **Timeline:** 2-3 business days
- **Criteria:**
  - Alert data extracted correctly (volume, timing, disposition)
  - Investigation procedures accurately documented
  - SLA calculations correct
  - Capacity assessment realistic

**Level 2: Compliance Review** (Security Manager, CISO)

- **Focus:** Policy compliance, SLA achievement, improvement plan adequacy
- **Timeline:** 3-5 business days
- **Criteria:**
  - SLA compliance meets policy requirements (>90% overall)
  - FP rate acceptable (<30%) or tuning plan in place
  - Investigation quality adequate
  - Escalation procedures meet regulatory requirements
  - Continuous improvement evidence

**Level 3: Executive Approval** (CISO)

- **Focus:** Strategic alignment, resource requirements, operational effectiveness
- **Timeline:** 5-7 business days
- **Criteria:**
  - Alert management capability demonstrates security operations effectiveness
  - Capacity vs. demand balanced (or expansion justified)
  - Response times support business risk tolerance
  - Investment in automation/SOAR justified if needed
  - Metrics support board reporting

**Total Approval Timeline:** 15 business days

**Post-Approval Actions:**
1. Extract tuning priorities from Sheet 2
2. Create detection engineering tasks
3. Develop missing investigation playbooks (Sheet 4 gaps)
4. Test escalation procedures (Sheet 5 gaps)
5. Implement continuous SLA tracking dashboard
6. Schedule next quarterly review

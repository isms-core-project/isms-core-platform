## Purpose and Scope

### Purpose

This implementation guide provides comprehensive procedures for assessing, documenting, and managing international transfers of personal data per GDPR Chapter V (Articles 44-50), Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) Article 16, and ISO/IEC 27001:2022 Control A.5.34 requirements.

### Assessment Objectives

1. **Transfer Inventory**: Identify and catalog ALL cross-border personal data transfers
2. **Legal Basis Validation**: Verify appropriate transfer mechanisms (Adequacy, SCCs, BCRs, DPF, Derogations)
3. **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)**: Evaluate risks to data subjects for non-adequate country transfers
4. **Processor Compliance**: Ensure Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) include transfer safeguards
5. **Gap Remediation**: Address unlawful or under-documented international transfers
6. **Regulatory Reporting**: Maintain audit-ready documentation for supervisory authorities

### Scope

**In Scope:**

- All personal data transfers crossing EU/EEA borders (GDPR jurisdiction)
- All personal data transfers from Switzerland (FADP jurisdiction)
- Transfers to processors, joint controllers, and independent third parties
- Automated transfers (APIs, integrations) and manual transfers (email, file sharing)
- Onward transfers (processor-to-subprocessor in third countries)
- Cloud services with multi-region data storage or processing
- Remote access to PII systems by personnel in third countries

**Out of Scope:**

- Purely domestic data transfers within EU/EEA or within Switzerland
- Transfers between EU/EEA member states (free movement of data per GDPR Article 45)
- Anonymized data (GDPR does not apply if truly anonymized per GDPR Recital 26)
- Data physically transported by data subjects themselves (e.g., employee traveling with laptop)

### Regulatory Context

**GDPR Chapter V Requirements:**

- **Article 44**: General principle - transfers only with adequate safeguards
- **Article 45**: Adequacy decisions (EU Commission approves countries with adequate protection)
- **Article 46**: Appropriate safeguards (SCCs, BCRs, codes of conduct, certifications)
- **Article 47**: Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs) for intra-group transfers
- **Article 49**: Derogations for specific situations (limited use, not routine transfers)
- **Article 50**: International cooperation for mutual administrative assistance

**Swiss FADP Requirements:**

- **Article 16**: Disclosure of personal data abroad (similar to GDPR but separate adequacy list)
- **Article 17**: Processor obligations (applies to processors outside Switzerland)
- Adequacy recognized by Swiss Federal Council (NOT identical to EU adequacy list)

**Post-Schrems II Landscape:**

- **Schrems II (2020)**: Invalidated EU-US Privacy Shield, requires case-by-case TIA for US transfers
- **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)**: Mandatory for transfers to countries without adequacy decision
- **Supplementary Measures**: Technical (encryption, pseudonymization) and contractual measures required
- **Continuous Monitoring**: Reassess TIAs if legal/political situation changes

## Assessment Preparation

### Prerequisites

Before starting, gather:

**1. PII Inventory Data (from A.5.34.1):**

- Sheet 2: PII Data Flow Mapping (source = cross-border transfers)
- Sheet 3: Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) with processor locations

**2. Processor Contracts:**

- Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) per GDPR Article 28
- Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) if processor in non-adequate country
- Subprocessor lists (if processor uses subprocessors in third countries)

**3. Technical Documentation:**

- Cloud service provider infrastructure maps (AWS regions, Azure datacenters)
- API integration documentation (where does data flow?)
- Remote access logs (VPN access from which countries?)
- Backup/DR configurations (are backups replicated internationally?)

**4. Legal Basis Documentation:**

- Consent records (if relying on Art. 49 derogations)
- Contract necessity evidence (if using Art. 49(1)(b) derogation)
- Legitimate interest assessments (if using Art. 49(1)(f) occasional transfer derogation)

**5. Regulatory Resources:**

- EU Commission adequacy decisions list (https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/international-dimension-data-protection/adequacy-decisions_en)
- Swiss Federal Council adequacy list (https://www.edoeb.admin.ch/edoeb/en/home/data-protection/handel-und-wirtschaft/transborder-data-flows.html)
- EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF) participant list (https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/s/participant-search)
- EDPB Guidelines on TIAs (https://edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/our-documents/recommendations/recommendations-012020-measures-supplement-transfer_en)

#### 2.3 Common Transfer Scenarios to Check

**Scenario 1: Cloud Services with Multi-Region Architecture**

- **Example:** AWS S3 bucket with replication to US-East-1 region
- **Transfer:** EU (primary) → US (replica) = cross-border transfer
- **Mechanism:** Review AWS DPA for SCCs, check if DPF certification applies

**Scenario 2: Remote Access by International Employees**

- **Example:** Software developer in India accessing EU-hosted database via VPN
- **Transfer:** EU database → India (accessed remotely) = cross-border transfer
- **Mechanism:** Assess if employment contract covers transfer, consider internal BCRs if multinational company

**Scenario 3: Processor Using Subprocessors in Third Countries**

- **Example:** EU processor (e.g., email service) using US subprocessor for spam filtering
- **Transfer:** EU processor → US subprocessor = onward transfer
- **Mechanism:** Verify processor's DPA includes subprocessor authorization, check if SCCs flow down

**Scenario 4: Marketing Tools and Analytics**

- **Example:** Google Analytics tracking website visitors
- **Transfer:** EU website → Google servers (US/worldwide) = cross-border transfer
- **Mechanism:** Check Google's DPA, review if Google Analytics 4 with EU data hosting option enabled

**Scenario 5: Customer Support Platforms**

- **Example:** Zendesk ticketing system
- **Transfer:** EU support tickets → Zendesk (US headquarters, but may have EU data residency option)
- **Mechanism:** Review Zendesk contract, verify if EU data hosting selected, check SCCs

### Step 3: Determine Transfer Mechanism (Sheet 2 continued)

For each transfer, identify the legal mechanism per GDPR Article 44-46:

#### 3.1 Check Adequacy Status (Column: "Adequacy Status")

**EU Adequacy Decisions (as of 2025):**

- Andorra, Argentina, Canada (commercial organizations), Faroe Islands, Guernsey, Israel, Isle of Man, Japan, Jersey, New Zealand, Republic of Korea, Switzerland, United Kingdom, Uruguay
- **EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)**: US companies that self-certify (check https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/s/participant-search)

**Swiss Adequacy List (separate from EU):**

- Similar to EU but NOT identical - check Swiss Federal Council decisions
- Notable: UK adequacy from Switzerland post-Brexit requires separate assessment

**If Destination Country = Adequate:**

- **Transfer Mechanism:** "Adequacy Decision (GDPR Art. 45)" or "EU-US DPF Certification"
- **TIA Required?** NO (adequacy decision means appropriate safeguards guaranteed)
- **Action:** Verify adequacy decision still valid (can be revoked)

**If Destination Country = NOT Adequate:**

- **Transfer Mechanism:** Must select appropriate safeguard per GDPR Article 46:
  - Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
  - Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)
  - Code of Conduct + Binding Enforceable Commitments
  - Certification Mechanism + Binding Enforceable Commitments
  - Derogation (Article 49 - only for non-routine, infrequent transfers)
- **TIA Required?** YES (Transfer Impact Assessment per Schrems II requirements)

#### 3.2 Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) Identification

**EU SCCs (2021 Modernized Version):**

- **Module 1:** Controller-to-Controller
- **Module 2:** Controller-to-Processor (most common for cloud services)
- **Module 3:** Processor-to-Processor (subprocessor relationships)
- **Module 4:** Processor-to-Controller (rare - e.g., analytics services)

**Action:** Review processor contracts for SCC annexes:
1. Check if SCCs present in DPA
2. Verify correct module used (Module 2 for most cloud processors)
3. Confirm SCCs are **2021 version** (old 2010 SCCs invalid as of Sept 2022)
4. Check if SCCs signed by both parties (not just included as exhibit)
5. Document **SCC Signature Date** in Sheet 2

**Swiss SCCs:**

- Switzerland has separate SCC templates (not identical to EU)
- If processing Swiss personal data, verify Swiss SCCs or adequacy applies

#### 3.3 Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)

**Applicability:** Multinational companies with intra-group transfers

**Verification:**

- Check if company has BCRs approved by EU supervisory authority
- BCRs must cover all entities in group involved in processing
- Document BCR approval date and approving authority

**Rare:** Most organizations use SCCs instead of BCRs (BCRs require multi-year approval process)

#### 3.4 Derogations (GDPR Article 49)

**Use with Caution:** Derogations are **last resort**, NOT routine transfer mechanisms

**When Derogations May Apply:**

| Derogation | Criteria | Example | Limitations |
|------------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Art. 49(1)(a): Explicit Consent** | Data subject explicitly consents to transfer after being informed of risks | User clicks "I consent to my data being transferred to US" after risk disclosure | Cannot be used for routine business transfers (e.g., all CRM transfers) |
| **Art. 49(1)(b): Contract Necessity** | Transfer necessary for contract between data subject and controller | Booking international flight (need to share PII with foreign airline) | Only for occasional contract execution, not ongoing business |
| **Art. 49(1)(c): Public Interest** | Transfer necessary for public interest or legal claims | International litigation, criminal investigations | Rare in commercial context |
| **Art. 49(1)(f): Legitimate Interest** | Transfer not repetitive, concerns limited number of data subjects, necessary for compelling legitimate interest | One-time M&A due diligence | CANNOT be used for routine, systematic transfers |

**Red Flags:**

- Using Art. 49 for ongoing, systematic transfers (e.g., "we use Art. 49 for all US transfers") = **UNLAWFUL**
- Claiming "legitimate interest" derogation for SaaS tool used daily = **UNLAWFUL**
- Blanket consent for all future transfers without specific risk disclosure = **INVALID**

**If Derogation Claimed:** Document detailed justification in Sheet 2 Notes field, link to legal assessment

### Step 4: Conduct Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) (Sheet 3)

**Requirement:** GDPR Article 46 + Schrems II CJEU decision mandate TIA for transfers to non-adequate countries

**When TIA Required:**

- Transfer to country WITHOUT EU adequacy decision
- Even if SCCs in place (SCCs alone insufficient post-Schrems II)
- US transfers especially scrutinized (FISA 702, EO 12333 surveillance concerns)

#### 4.1 TIA Methodology (Sheet 3 Assessment)

For each transfer requiring TIA, evaluate:

**Step 1: Assess Destination Country Legal Framework**

| Factor | Questions to Assess | Red Flags |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Surveillance Laws** | Does country have laws allowing mass surveillance of communications? | US FISA Section 702, UK Investigatory Powers Act, China Cybersecurity Law |
| **Data Access Powers** | Can government demand access to personal data without judicial oversight? | US CLOUD Act, China National Security Law |
| **Data Subject Rights** | Can data subjects enforce their rights in destination country courts? | No effective judicial redress mechanism |
| **Onward Transfer Restrictions** | Are there restrictions on further international transfers? | China cross-border transfer approval requirements |
| **Independent Oversight** | Is there independent data protection authority? | Countries without DPA or DPA lacks enforcement power |

**Step 2: Assess Importer's Specific Circumstances**

| Factor | Questions to Assess | Risk Mitigation |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Importer Type** | Is importer subject to surveillance laws? (Telecom providers often subject to more surveillance than other sectors) | If yes, supplementary measures required |
| **Data Access Requests History** | Has importer received government data access requests? | Check transparency reports |
| **Contractual Commitments** | Does importer commit to challenge unlawful requests? | Verify "challenge clause" in DPA |
| **Technical Measures** | Are there encryption/pseudonymization measures preventing importer access? | End-to-end encryption with client-held keys |
| **Data Localization Options** | Can data be stored exclusively in adequate jurisdiction? | AWS EU-only regions, Azure EU residency |

**Step 3: Identify Supplementary Measures**

If TIA reveals risks, implement supplementary measures:

**Technical Measures:**

- **Encryption in transit:** TLS 1.3 (minimum)
- **Encryption at rest:** AES-256 with customer-managed keys (not provider-managed)
- **End-to-end encryption:** Provider cannot decrypt data (e.g., client-side encryption)
- **Pseudonymization:** Replace identifiers with pseudonyms (reversible only by data exporter)
- **Data minimization:** Transfer only necessary PII fields
- **Access controls:** Restrict importer personnel access to minimum necessary
- **Multi-party encryption:** Split key approach requiring multiple parties to decrypt

**Contractual Measures:**

- **Obligation to challenge:** Importer must challenge unlawful government data access requests
- **Transparency:** Importer must notify exporter of government requests (unless legally prohibited)
- **Suspension clause:** Exporter can suspend transfers if legal framework changes
- **Audit rights:** Exporter can audit importer's compliance with safeguards
- **Data return/deletion:** Upon termination, importer returns or deletes all data

**Organizational Measures:**

- **Data governance:** Clear accountability for transfer compliance
- **Staff training:** Importer personnel trained on GDPR obligations
- **Incident response:** Procedures for government access request scenarios
- **Regular monitoring:** Quarterly review of destination country legal developments

#### 4.2 TIA Documentation in Sheet 3

| Column | What to Document | Example |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| **TIA ID** | Unique identifier | "TIA-2025-001" |
| **Transfer ID** | Link to Sheet 2 | "XFER-2025-001" |
| **Destination Country** | Target country | "United States" |
| **Adequacy Status** | EU/Swiss adequacy | "No EU Adequacy (US not adequate post-Schrems II)" |
| **Surveillance Laws** | Identified risks | "FISA 702, EO 12333, CLOUD Act" |
| **Government Access Risk** | Likelihood × Impact | "Medium (electronic communications subject to FISA 702)" |
| **Importer Type** | Cloud provider, telecom, other | "Cloud Infrastructure Provider (not telecommunications)" |
| **Data Access Requests History** | Check transparency report | "Provider publishes transparency report - no FISA requests disclosed (likely under gag order)" |
| **Supplementary Measures (Technical)** | Encryption, pseudonymization | "TLS 1.3 in transit, AES-256 at rest with AWS KMS (customer-managed keys), access logging enabled" |
| **Supplementary Measures (Contractual)** | DPA clauses | "AWS DPA includes obligation to challenge requests, notification clause (subject to gag order limitations)" |
| **Supplementary Measures (Organizational)** | Governance, monitoring | "Quarterly review of AWS security bulletins, annual DPA compliance audit" |
| **TIA Conclusion** | Pass/Fail with conditions | "PASS with supplementary measures: Transfer lawful subject to continued use of customer-managed encryption keys and quarterly monitoring" |
| **TIA Approval** | DPO sign-off | "Approved by DPO [Name] on [Date]" |
| **Review Date** | Next reassessment | "Review by Q1 2026 or upon legal/political change" |

#### 4.3 TIA Special Cases

**US Transfers Post-Schrems II:**

- **EU-US DPF:** If importer certified under Data Privacy Framework, adequacy applies (TIA not required)
- **Non-DPF US Transfers:** TIA required, focus on FISA 702/EO 12333 risks
- **US Government Contracts:** Extremely high risk - consider if transfer can be avoided

**China Transfers:**

- High risk due to National Security Law, Cybersecurity Law, Data Security Law
- Cross-Border Transfer Security Assessment (CBTSA) required for large volumes
- Consider alternatives (process data in Hong Kong/Singapore instead if possible)

**UK Post-Brexit:**

- UK has EU adequacy decision (valid until June 2025, renewable)
- Monitor for adequacy decision renewal
- If adequacy lapses, UK transfers require SCCs + TIA

### Step 5: Verify Processor Agreements (Sheet 4)

**Objective:** Ensure Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) include transfer safeguards

#### 5.1 For Each Processor Transfer, Check

| Requirement | Where to Find | Red Flag |
|-------------|--------------|----------|
| **DPA Exists** | Contract annexes, procurement records | No signed DPA = GDPR Art. 28 violation |
| **DPA is GDPR-Compliant** | DPA references GDPR Art. 28 obligations | Generic "confidentiality agreement" insufficient |
| **Processor Location Identified** | DPA lists processing locations | Vague "worldwide" without specific countries |
| **Subprocessor List Included** | DPA Annex or separate schedule | No subprocessor disclosure |
| **SCCs Included (if non-adequate country)** | DPA Annex with signed SCCs | Missing SCCs for US/other non-adequate processors |
| **SCCs are 2021 Version** | Check SCC date and module | Using old 2010 SCCs (invalid after Sept 27, 2022) |
| **Subprocessor SCCs Flow Down** | DPA requires processor to impose SCCs on subprocessors | Processor uses subprocessors without SCCs |
| **TIA Documentation** | Reference to completed TIA | No TIA for non-adequate country transfer |
| **Data Return/Deletion Clause** | DPA includes post-termination obligations | No data deletion commitment |
| **Audit Rights** | DPA allows controller to audit processor | No audit clause or limited audit rights |

### Step 7: Gap Analysis (Sheet 6)

**Objective:** Identify non-compliant or under-documented transfers for remediation

#### 7.1 Common Gaps to Check For

| Gap Type | Description | Risk Level | Example |
|----------|-------------|------------|---------|
| **Undocumented Transfer** | Transfer occurring but not in register | **CRITICAL** | Marketing team using US tool without Legal/DPO approval |
| **Missing SCCs** | Transfer to non-adequate country without SCCs | **CRITICAL** | US processor processing PII but DPA lacks SCCs |
| **No TIA** | Transfer to non-adequate country without TIA | **HIGH** | Using AWS US regions but never conducted Schrems II TIA |
| **Old SCCs** | Using 2010 SCCs (invalid since Sept 2022) | **HIGH** | DPA signed in 2018 with old SCC version, never updated |
| **Inadequate Supplementary Measures** | TIA identifies risks but no mitigation | **HIGH** | TIA flags surveillance risk but no encryption implemented |
| **No DPA** | Processor processing PII without GDPR Art. 28 agreement | **CRITICAL** | Outsourced support team processing tickets without DPA |
| **Unlawful Derogation Use** | Using Art. 49 for routine transfers | **CRITICAL** | Claiming "legitimate interest" derogation for daily SaaS transfers |
| **Subprocessor Without SCCs** | Processor uses subprocessor in third country without SCCs | **HIGH** | EU processor uses US spam filtering service without onward transfer SCCs |

## Post-Assessment Activities

### Remediation Kick-Off

Once Sheet 6 (Gap Analysis) is complete:

1. **Prioritize Critical and High Gaps**

   - Critical gaps = stop transfers immediately until remediated (if feasible) OR document explicit risk acceptance by senior management
   - High gaps = remediate within 1-3 months

2. **Assign Remediation Owners**

   - Legal: Negotiate SCCs with processors
   - IT/Security: Implement technical supplementary measures
   - Procurement: Identify alternative processors in adequate jurisdictions
   - Business Owners: Approve changes to business processes

3. **Set Up Tracking Meetings**

   - Weekly for Critical gaps
   - Bi-weekly for High gaps
   - Monthly for Medium/Low gaps

4. **Escalation Procedures**

   - If Critical gap not resolved in 4 weeks → escalate to CISO + Legal Counsel
   - If High gap not resolved in 3 months → escalate to Privacy Committee
   - If persistent non-compliance → consider suspending transfer per GDPR Art. 46 obligations

### Ongoing Monitoring (Quarterly)

1. **Review TIAs** (Sheet 3)

   - Check if destination country legal framework changed (new surveillance laws, adequacy revoked)
   - Update TIA conclusion if risks increased
   - Reassess supplementary measures effectiveness

2. **Monitor Processor Compliance** (Sheet 4)

   - Review processor transparency reports
   - Check if processor added new subprocessors (should be notified per DPA)
   - Conduct audits per DPA audit clause (annually or bi-annually)

3. **Update Transfer Register** (Sheet 2)

   - Add new transfers (new SaaS tools, new processors)
   - Remove terminated transfers
   - Update transfer volumes and frequencies

4. **Gap Remediation Progress** (Sheet 6)

   - Update gap status (Open → In Progress → Completed)
   - Close completed gaps
   - Escalate overdue gaps

5. **Dashboard Review** (Sheet 7)

   - Present to Privacy Committee quarterly
   - Track trends (are gaps increasing or decreasing?)
   - Celebrate wins (gaps closed, TIAs completed)

### Annual Reassessment

**Timing:** Q4 (October-December) to align with budget planning

1. **Complete Reassessment:**

   - Re-inventory all transfers (systems change, new tools adopted)
   - Re-validate transfer mechanisms (adequacy decisions can change)
   - Re-conduct TIAs for high-risk transfers

2. **Regulatory Updates:**

   - Check for new EU Commission adequacy decisions
   - Review EDPB guidance updates
   - Monitor supervisory authority enforcement actions (learn from others' mistakes)

3. **Processor Audits:**

   - Exercise audit rights per DPAs
   - Review SOC 2 / ISO 27001 audit reports from processors
   - Verify supplementary measures still in place

4. **Training:**

   - Train Legal, IT, Privacy teams on GDPR Chapter V updates
   - Educate business owners on cross-border transfer requirements

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### Pitfall 1: Assuming Cloud = Adequate

**Mistake:** "We use AWS EU regions, so no cross-border transfers."

**Reality:**

- AWS support teams may access data from US
- AWS Backup may replicate to US regions if not configured correctly
- Remote access by AWS engineers from non-EU locations

**Solution:** Review cloud service provider DPA, confirm data residency guarantees, check backup/DR configurations

---

### Pitfall 2: Ignoring Remote Access

**Mistake:** "Data stays in EU database, so it's fine."

**Reality:** If employee in India VPNs in and accesses EU database, that's a cross-border transfer (data accessed from India)

**Solution:** Map remote access patterns, consider VDI (Virtual Desktop Infrastructure) to keep data in EU while allowing remote viewing

---

### Pitfall 3: Blanket Derogations

**Mistake:** "We use Article 49 legitimate interest derogation for all US transfers."

**Reality:** Art. 49 derogations ONLY for non-routine, occasional transfers. Cannot be used for systematic transfers (e.g., daily SaaS sync).

**Solution:** Use SCCs + TIA for routine transfers, reserve derogations for truly exceptional cases

---

### Pitfall 4: Old SCCs

**Mistake:** "We have SCCs from 2019 in our DPA, we're covered."

**Reality:** Old 2010 SCCs invalid since September 27, 2022. Must update to 2021 SCCs.

**Solution:** Audit all processor DPAs, request SCC amendments to 2021 version

---

### Pitfall 5: No TIA

**Mistake:** "We have SCCs, that's enough."

**Reality:** Schrems II requires TIA for non-adequate countries EVEN WITH SCCs.

**Solution:** Conduct TIA for every non-adequate transfer, document supplementary measures

---

### Pitfall 6: Subprocessor Blind Spot

**Mistake:** "Our processor is in EU, so no cross-border transfer."

**Reality:** If EU processor uses US subprocessor (e.g., spam filtering), that's an onward transfer requiring SCCs.

**Solution:** Review processor's subprocessor list, ensure SCCs flow down to all subprocessors

---

### Pitfall 7: Expired DPF Certification

**Mistake:** "We checked vendor was DPF certified in 2023, we're good."

**Reality:** DPF certifications expire annually, must be renewed.

**Solution:** Check https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/s/participant-search quarterly, verify certification current

## Definitions and Terminology

| Term | Definition | GDPR Reference |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **Adequacy Decision** | EU Commission decision recognizing a third country provides adequate level of data protection | Article 45 |
| **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** | Commission-approved contractual clauses providing appropriate safeguards for transfers | Article 46(2)(c) |
| **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** | Internal rules for multinational companies allowing intra-group transfers | Article 47 |
| **Derogation** | Exception allowing transfer in specific situations (consent, contract necessity, etc.) - last resort | Article 49 |
| **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)** | Assessment of risks to data subjects from transfer to third country (Schrems II requirement) | EDPB Recommendations 01/2020 |
| **Supplementary Measures** | Additional technical/organizational measures beyond SCCs to ensure adequate protection | Schrems II + EDPB Recommendations 01/2020 |
| **EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)** | Adequacy framework for US organizations that self-certify compliance | Article 45 (via Commission Adequacy Decision) |
| **Onward Transfer** | Transfer by processor to subprocessor in third country | Article 46 (SCCs must flow down) |
| **Third Country** | Country outside EU/EEA (Switzerland also considered third country relative to EU) | Article 4(26) |
| **International Organization** | Organization subject to public international law (e.g., UN, WHO) | Article 4(26) |
